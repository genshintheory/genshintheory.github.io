The ball will move in the direction opposite of a player. The direction is given by the unit vector from the player to the ball. If there are multiple players, these unit vectors are summed, giving the direction the ball will move in.

Suppose we just care about the player's xy coordinates. Suppose a player i's position in 2-space is given by \(u_i \in R^2 \). Suppose the ball's position is given by \(x \in R^2 \). The ball will move in the direction given by: $$\sum_i (x-u_i)$$

To write the dynamics $$x(t+dt) = x(t) + dt \frac{\sum_i (x(t)-u_i(t))}{\sum_i |x(t)-u_i(t)|}$$ where dt is an 'infinitesimal'. The above difference equation can be readily programmed into a discrete form for computers, taking dt to be a suitably small icd/fps/clocking interval.

We can also take the continuous limit and write the differential equation: $$\frac{dx}{dt} = \frac{\sum_i (x(t)-u_i(t))}{\sum_i |x(t)-u_i(t)|}$$ We normalize so that the ball has constant speed. It is just that its direction can change due to the movements of the other players u.

Assuming Newtonian physics, we can derive the kind of force exerted by the players: $$F = ma = m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = m\frac{d}{dt} \frac{\sum_i (x(t)-u_i(t))}{\sum_i |x(t)-u_i(t)|}$$ Usually masses attract due to gravity, but in this event there is some kind of repellant force between player and ball.
